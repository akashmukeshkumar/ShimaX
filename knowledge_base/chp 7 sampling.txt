📚 LECTURE FULL EXPLANATION:
🔹 Main Concept:
Topic: Selection Techniques in Audit
Aim: auditor ko guide krna ke kaun se method use krna for sampling & selection taki misstatements detect ki ja saken.
🔹 Discussion Start:
Pension ka case → conclusion ye nikla ke public information hai aur management ko properly record krna chahiye.
Auditor ki responsibility: har condition ko evaluate karna + professional skepticism apply krna.
🔹 Selection Techniques Overview:
Selection ka concept har jagah audit me use hota hai:
Receivables → confirmations bhejni.
Sales → transactions select krna.
Fixed Assets → existence verify krna.
Ye chapter auditor ko 10 selection techniques sikhata hai.
Important Point: chapter sirf idea deta hai → actual method depend karega situation per.
👉 Line yaad rakhni hai: “Application situation to situation change hoti hai.”
🔹 Practical Example (Bank Loan Book)
Ek bank ka loan book audit ho raha tha.
Books me saare loans performing show kiye gaye (koi overdue nahi).
Auditor ko suspicion hua → business knowledge se farmers loans usually default karte hain.
Auditor ne kya kiya?
Pura loan book 2019 se uthaya.
Sampling nahi ki → full population test apply kiya.
Finding: kuch loans actually Non-Performing Loans (NPLs) thay but performings show kiye gaye.
🔹 Concept Highlight (NPL)
Non-Performing Loan (NPL): aisa loan jo borrower wapis time pe repay nahi karta.
Banks kabhi kabhi NPLs ko performing dikhate hain taki provisioning zyada na lage.
🔹 Key Learning:
Auditor ko apni business understanding + professional judgment use krni chahiye.
Agar suspicion ho → normal sampling ke bajaye full population test karna chahiye.
Risk: misclassification → material misstatement create karega.
👉 Example se link: bank loan book case me selection technique auditor ne change ki situation ke hisaab se (from sampling → full population).
🔹 Practical Example (Loan Book Analysis – Farmers Loans)
Auditor ne client se 2019–2024 ki loan books mangwai.
Excel aur unique Loan ID (Customer ID) use karke data track kiya year to year.
Observation:
Same Loan ID multiple saalon me chal rahi thi.
Farmers loans usually max 2 saal ke liye diye jate hain, lekin yahan 5 saal tak extend ho rahe the.
Maturity Dates bar bar change ho rahi thi (2019 me maturity 2021, phir 2023, phir 2025, hatta ke 2028).
Finding: management loans ki maturity dates artificially change karke NPL ko performing dikhati thi.
Result: ek 6 billion ka loan mila jisme provision book nahi hua tha.
👉 Ye fraud auditor ne 100% population testing se pakra, kyunki high risk tha + CAATs ka use karke cost effective tha.
🔹 100% Selection Kab?
High Risk Area → fraud ya misstatement ka chance zyada ho.
Cost-Effective via CAATs → jab software tools available ho aur pura data analyze karna easy ho.
Small Number of Large Values → jaise receivables me kuch hi customers 60% balance hold karte hain.
👉 Example: agar receivables population me 500 customers hain, aur top 50 customers 60% hold karte hain → un sab pe 100% confirmation bhejna justified hai.
🔹 Specific Selection (Apart from 100%)
Kabhi auditor specific items select karta hai jo unusual ya high risk lagte hain:
Items above certain value (e.g., invoices above threshold).
Items showing special characteristics (e.g., unusual debit balances in sales ledger).
Transactions near year-end (cut-off testing).
👉 Example: Sales ledger me agar normally credit balances hain, lekin kuch debit balances mile, to unko specifically select karna hoga.

🔹 Recap from Last Class:
Last class me auditor ko selection techniques sikhayi gayi thi.
Sabse pehle check karna hota hai: 100% selection possible hai ya nahi?
Small number of large values ho → 100% selection.
Agar cost-effective ho via CAATs → 100% selection.
Agar high risk ho → 100% selection.
Lekin har jagah 100% check practical nahi hota (e.g., PTCL revenue – automation bohot zyada → high risk hote hue bhi sampling karna padta hai, lekin bada sample size).
🔹 Specific Selection (Recap + Detail):
Auditor kabhi kabhi specific items uthata hai unusual ya risky hone ki wajah se:
Sales ledger normally credit balances hoti hain, lekin debit balances aaye → specifically select karne hain.
Fixed asset register me negative items / unusual balances → specifically check karne hain.
Period-end ke kareeb wali transactions (cut-off testing) → specifically select karne hain.
👉 Ye sab specific selection ke examples hain.
🔹 Untested Population:
Jo items 100% check nahi hue aur jo specifically select nahi hue, wo bachi population kehlati hai = Untested Population.
Untested population par auditor sampling apply karta hai.
🔹 Link with Audit Risk:
Audit ke shuru me auditor audit risk model samajhta hai:
Audit Risk = Inherent Risk × Control Risk × Detection Risk
Understanding phase me auditor ko entity aur uske environment ki knowledge leni padti hai.
Ye knowledge help karti hai inherent risks identify karne me.
🔹 Inherent Risk (IR):
Definition: aise risks jo entity ke nature, industry, ya balances ki wajah se naturally exist karte hain.
Examples:
Pharmaceutical company: Inventory ka NRV risk high hota hai (expiry issue → inherent risk).
Bank: Cash balances branch locations pe scattered hote hain → misappropriation ka risk = inherent risk.
PTCL: Pension note ka risk → disclosure me misstatement ka chance (nature of disclosure → inherent risk).
👉 Inherent risk ka matlab hai “ye risk exist karega chahe controls ho ya na ho.”
🔹 Audit Risk Understanding via Examples:
Industry-based IR → Pharma industry me inventory NRV risk.
Nature of Balance IR → PTCL ka pension disclosure, ya static balances jo doubtful lagte hain.
Nature of Operations IR → Bank me cash handling ka risk.
Going Concern IR → Agar company losses aur borrowings me phase hui ho, to going concern ka disclosure high risk area ban jata hai.
🔹 Detection Risk (DR):
DR = chance ke auditor misstatement detect na kar paaye.
Agar Audit Risk zyada ho jata hai (IR high + Controls weak), to auditor ko apna Detection Risk kam karna parta hai.
Matlab: auditor ko zyada kaam karna hoga (sample size barhao, procedures strengthen karo).
👉 Example: Inventory ka NRV Loss.
NRV Loss inherent risk hai.
Agar is par koi control nahi hai → Audit Risk high.
Result: Auditor ka DR kam hona chahiye → zyada testing karo.
🔹 Detection Risk Manage Karne ke Do Tools:
Sampling Risk Control
Risk ke sample me galat item miss ho jaye.
Mitigate by:
Sample size increase karna.
Population ko stratify karna (sub-populations bana lena).
Example: Inventory aging report me “slow moving items” alag karna.
Non-Sampling Risk Control
Jab sample me item aa jaye lekin auditor phir bhi error detect na kare.
Mitigate by:
Proper supervision.
Experienced staff ka use.
Scepticism maintain karna.
Example: Farmers loan case – auditor ko client ki justification blindly accept nahi karni.
🔹 Stratification Example:
Flood-affected inventory ko alag population treat karna.
Pure Pakistan ke warehouses audit karne ke bajaye → flood-hit areas specifically stratify karke test karo.
Is se error detect karne ka chance barhta hai (DR kam hota hai).

📚 LECTURE FULL EXPLANATION:
🔹 Recap & Link:
Last class me humne Audit Risk Model discuss kiya tha:
Audit Risk = IR × CR × DR.
Ab focus shift hua hai → Detection Risk (DR) ke components aur sampling application.
🔹 Detection Risk Breakdown:
DR ke 2 main components:
Sampling Risk
Non-Sampling Risk
Auditor ka kaam: dono risks ko control karna → taake misstatement detect na hone ka chance kam ho jaye.
🔹 Sampling Risk:
Sampling risk = jab sample population ko properly represent nahi karta.
Example:
Expenses ledger 17,14,000 ka hai.
Auditor ne 60,000 ka sample select kiya.
Usme ek invoice 10,250 ki galat nikli (wrong period booked).
Error rate = 10,250 / 1,21,000 (sample base) ≈ 8.4%.
Projected to population → ≈ 1,44,895 ki galti ho sakti hai.
Materiality threshold 1 million hai → yeh error abhi material nahi, lekin agar aur errors mil jayein to ho sakti hai.
👉 Learning: sampling ka result population par project karna hota hai.
🔹 Non-Sampling Risk:
Non-sampling risk = jab auditor ko error mil jata hai sample me lekin wo detect nahi kar pata ya ignore kar deta hai.
Example:
Agriculture loan rollover case.
Client bola “humne loan roll-over kiya hai” → auditor ne blindly accept kar liya.
Actually wo maturity change karke fraud kar rahe the.
Error auditor ke saamne tha, lekin detect nahi hua = non-sampling risk.
👉 Isko mitigate karne ke liye:
Experienced staff use karo.
Proper supervision do.
Professional scepticism apply karo.
🔹 Stratification in Sampling:
Population ko homogeneous sub-groups me todna.
Taake auditor ko representative aur clear picture mile.
Stratification bases:
Region-wise (e.g., Punjab vs Sindh).
Customer class-wise (wholesaler vs retailer).
Time-period wise (month-wise sales).
👉 Example: Receivables ko stratify karna based on customer type → wholesalers separately, retailers separately.
🔹 Homogeneity Concept:
Sample tabhi reliable hai jab population homogeneous ho.
Example:
Sales aur Sales Returns ko same population me include karna galat hoga.
Kyunki dono ka nature alag hai → population homogeneous nahi.
🔹 Sampling Error Projection Case:
Example me auditor ne expenses ledger ka ek sample check kiya.
Sample me galti detect hui aur phir usko project kiya population par.
Auditor ka kaam: project karna taake samajh aaye total misstatement kitni ho sakti hai.
👉 Case 1:
Ek invoice me error tha = 10,250.
Sample base ≈ 1,21,000 → error rate ≈ 8.4%.
Projected error ≈ 1,44,895.
Materiality = 1 million.
Result: Error material nahi → opinion unmodified rehta.
👉 Case 2:
Agar aur invoices galat hoti (e.g., 2–3 aur detect hoti).
Sample base barhta aur error % bhi barhta.
Projected misstatement ≈ 8,96,434 aa sakti thi.
Ab yeh materiality 5 lakh se upar chali gayi.
Result: Auditor ko apni audit strategy change karni parti.
🔹 Learning from Case:
Agar sampling galat ki gayi (jaise block sampling) → representative picture nahi milti.
Risk: auditor ka opinion inappropriate ho jata.
Correct approach = Random Selection (not block).
Agar projected misstatement materiality se exceed kar jaye → auditor ko:
Sample size barhana hoga.
Extended testing karni hogi.
Audit strategy revise karni hogi.
🔹 Block Sampling vs Random Sampling:
Block Sampling = ek sequence ya continuous block of invoices select karna → risk high hai ke population represent na ho.
Random Sampling = har item ko equal chance dena → zyada reliable.
👉 Exam me likhna: Auditor ne block sampling use ki jo inappropriate thi, correct method random selection tha.
🔁 Don’t mix stages (Planning vs Evaluation)
Planning time: IR × CR dekh kar DR target set hota hai → isi se initial sample size decide hota hai (zyada risk ⇒ bada sample, stratification, CAATs, etc.).
Evaluation time: Testing ke baad jo misstatements milti hain unko aggregate + project karke performance materiality (PM) / tolerable misstatement se compare karte hain.
TL;DR: Pehle se bada sample rakhna IR/CR ki wajah se hota hai; baad me agar projected misstatement PM se upar nikal jaye to extended work karna padta hai.
📈 Jab Projected Misstatement > Performance Materiality
Extended procedures:
Sample size increase karo (same stratum par jahan se error nikla).
Stratify / re‑stratify: high‑risk buckets (e.g., slow‑moving, flood‑hit region, old aging) alag karke targeted testing.
Actual misstatements (jo sample/specific se mili) + Projected misstatement ko aggregate karo.
Management se adjustments demand karo.
Agar adjust nahi karte aur aggregate > materiality:
Modify opinion (material/pervasive logic ke mutabiq). Scope limitation nahi—ye misstatement case hai.
✅ Jab Projected Misstatement ≤ Performance Materiality
Phir bhi identified errors management se correct karwao taake projection aur niche aa jaye.
Extra testing ki zarurat nahi, agar risk indicators stable hain.
🎯 Kya Project Karna Hai (and kya nahi)
Project only:
Jo homogeneous stratum me sampling se nikla error (e.g., Stationery expense ledger ka sampled bucket).
Project nahi:
Specific selection / 100% tested items (e.g., items > PM, unusual debits in sales ledger, period‑end cut‑off items). Inki misstatements actual ke tor par direct add hoti hain, project nahi hoti.
📌 Kahaan Sample Badhao?
Ussi remaining stratum me jahan se projection upar gaya hai (“below‑PM remainder”).
Jo items pehle se 100% check ho chuke (above PM / specific characteristics) — unpar sample nahi badhate; wo already covered hain.
🧭 Workflow (Cheat‑Sheet)
Set thresholds: Overall materiality → Performance Materiality (PM) → account‑level tolerable misstatement.
Specific selection:
Items > PM or special characteristics (debit balances in sales, negative/abnormal items, period‑end cut‑off) → 100% test.
Define untested population (homogeneous remainder) → sample (random/systematic/stratified).
Compute:
Error rate = (misstatement in sample) / (sample amount).
Projected misstatement = error rate × population amount (of that stratum).
Total likely misstatement = projected + actual (specific/100% items) − adjustments accepted by management.
Compare with PM / tolerable; if > PM → extend work + ask adjustments; else conclude.
Final aggregation vs Overall Materiality for opinion.
🔑 Core Concepts:
Sample Size & Errors
Jab sample size barhaya, errors nikal aayi → iska matlab sampling se issues trace huwe.
Agar errors correct kar liye gaye → kahani wahan khatam ho jati hai ✅
Agar errors correct nahi huwe → phir aur test karna hoga (increase sampling / extend procedures).
Performance Materiality vs. Materiality Threshold
Performance Materiality (PM) = materiality se neeche ek buffer line.
Agar error PM ke neeche hai → immaterial.
Agar error PM se upar hai → ye important ban jata hai aur Summary of Uncorrected Misstatement me jata hai.
Projection of Errors (Sample → Population)
Sample me errors milay → unko project karke pura population ka estimate banta hai.
Projection sirf homogeneous items par hoti hai.
Agar projected error bhi materiality threshold se neeche hai → kuch further kaam nahi karna.
Agar projected error materiality se upar hai → auditor modify opinion / further test karega.
Types of Misstatements to Consider
Actual misstatements → jo 100% tested items me directly mile.
Projected misstatements → sample errors ko project karke population ka estimate.
Summary of Uncorrected Misstatements (SUOM) = Actual + Projected misstatements.
Auditor’s Conclusion
Agar SUOM < Materiality → Opinion = Unmodified
Agar SUOM ≥ Materiality → Opinion = Qualified/Modified
Auditor management ko sabhi misstatements (material & immaterial) correct karne ke liye bolega. Lekin agar management refuse kare → conclusion materiality ke comparison se hoga.

📚 LECTURE FULL EXPLANATION:
🔹 Entry Point (Link from Previous Lecture)
Pichli class me humne sampling error projection discuss kiya tha.
Ab focus: Jab misstatement sample me mile aur uska nature samajhna (systematic vs isolated).
🔹 Management Correction vs Projection
Auditor ne sample test kiya → error mila.
Scenario 1: Agar management ne wo specific error correct kar diya (e.g., ek invoice misbooked thi, rectify kar di).
Tab us error ko project karne ki zarurat nahi. ✅
Scenario 2: Agar error ek system issue ki wajah se hua (e.g., 4 din ka booking system faulty tha).
Tab auditor ko further test karna hoga, taake confirm ho jaye ke baaki dinon me bhi same error nahi hua.
👉 Key: Agar error isolated hai → no projection. Agar error systematic hai → projection / further testing.
🔹 Example: Salary Tax Deduction
Population: Tax withheld by system from employees’ salaries (not salaries themselves!).
Auditor ka objective: Verify kiya system ne correct tax deduct kiya ya nahi.
Sampling me 4 employees ke tax check kiye → 2 me error tha.
Case 1:
Error March & April ke months me tha (jab tax officer leave pe tha).
Auditor ne verify kiya ke ye sirf 2 months ke liye issue tha.
Further testing ne confirm kiya → baaki months correct the.
Result: Projection ki zarurat nahi, sirf March–April errors record kar liye.
Case 2:
Agar system error poori saal me consistently hota → to auditor us error ko population par project karta.
🔹 Performance Materiality vs Actual Errors
Auditor hamesha errors ko Summarize of Uncorrected Misstatements (SUOM) me daalta hai.
Chahe error PM se neeche ho, phir bhi note kiya jata hai (documenting requirement).
Lekin opinion par effect tab hoga jab Aggregate (Actual + Projected) > Materiality.
🔹 Workflow Recap (for Projection Decision)
Error mila sample me → pehle dekho ye isolated hai ya systemic.
Isolated → management correct kare, aur projection ki zarurat nahi.
Systemic / recurring → projection karo, aur agar projected error > PM → extra testing karo.
Aggregate actual + projected → SUOM me add karo.
Compare vs Materiality → opinion decide karo.
🔹 Sampling Projection vs Management Justification
Jab auditor sample se error detect karta hai → usko population par project karta hai.
Lekin agar management justification provide kare (e.g., “yeh error sirf 2 mahine ki anomaly hai”) → auditor ko validate karna hota hai:
Agar justification valid hai → projection reduce ho sakta hai.
Agar justification invalid hai → projection as it is population par apply hoga.
👉 Example: Auditor ne 2 invoices galat payin. Management bola: “Ye sirf flood ke 2 mahine ka effect hai.” Auditor verify karega:
Valid justification → sirf us 2 month bucket par error project hoga.
Invalid justification → poore stratum / population par projection apply hoga.
🔹 Auditor’s Evidence Flow
Sample test karo → error detect hua.
Management explanation lo.
Explanation validate karo:
Agar valid hai → misstatement restricted rahegi.
Agar valid nahi hai → error ko project karke population ka estimate banao.
👉 Har case me auditor sufficient appropriate audit evidence (SAAE) collect karta hai.
🔹 Population vs Sample Treatment
Jab auditor 100% population test karta hai → projection ki zarurat nahi.
Jab auditor sample test karta hai → projection mandatory hai (unless management ne valid justification de di ho).
🔹 Extended Procedures Scenario
Agar management justification valid nahi hoti → auditor kya karega?
Errors ko project karega.
Aggregate karega with other actual + projected misstatements.
Compare karega Performance Materiality (PM) se.
Agar exceed karta hai → aur testing (sample size barhaye, re-stratification, targeted testing) karega.
👉 Agar management correction kar deti hai:
Auditor wapas se ledger re-check karega.
Purane detected errors ko verify karega.
Fir naya sample select karke check karega.
Agar phir errors nahi milti → conclude karega ki misstatements corrected hain.
🔹 Integrity Doubts
Agar management bar-bar galat justification deta hai ya corrections reliable nahi lagte:
Auditor ke liye integrity doubt create hota hai.
Result: Auditor apni testing aur strict karega.
Agar doubt severe hai → opinion modification tak ja sakta hai.
🔹 Core Theme
Auditor ka kaam hai: Sample test → Error detect → Project → Evaluate with Management Explanation.
Key Question: “Summary of Uncorrected Misstatements (SUOM) mein kya le kar jaoge?”
Answer: Actual misstatements + Projected misstatements (unless 100% testing ho).
🔹 Sample Error & Projection
Sample error detect hota hai → population par project karna mandatory hai.
Exception: Jab 100% population check hoti hai → projection ki zarurat nahi hoti (sirf actual misstatement record hota hai).
👉 Example:
Sample error = 41.
Population size = large (say 666k).
Projection = (Sample error ÷ Sample size) × Population size.
Resulting projected misstatement add hota hai SUOM mein.
🔹 Management Correction & Projection
Agar management errors correct kar deti hai → auditor re-check karega.
Agar re-check ke baad sab sahi nikla → SUOM mein include nahi hoga.
Agar correction valid nahi hui → auditor projected misstatement include karega.
👉 Rule:
Corrected misstatement → SUOM mein nahi jata.
Uncorrected misstatement (actual + projected) → SUOM mein jata hai.
🔹 Sampling in Different Tests
Sampling hoti hai:
Test of Controls (TOC) → e.g., New customer ki credit limit set hui ya nahi?
Test of Details (TOD) → e.g., Confirmations bhejna for balances.
👉 Example:
TOC: New customer onboarding par credit limit approval check karna.
Population: New customers onboarded during the year.
TOD: Debtor confirmations.
Population: Receivables listing (AR balance schedule).
🔹 Purpose & Population
Purpose (Objective): Define karo pehle – TOC or TOD?
Population: Wo dataset jahan se sample uthana hai.
Agar population galat select hui → pura sample aur projection galat hoga → audit opinion wrong ho sakta hai.
Lecture- 5
📚 Reference: Book Pages -
📚 LECTURE FULL EXPLANATION:
🔹 Core Issue in the Question
Scenario me auditor ne 25 write-off cases select kiye aur unpe procedures perform kiye (board approval, correspondence check, etc.).
Lekin yeh approach galat population se sample uthana tha.
👉 Reason:
Write-off ho chuke balances to already books se eliminate ho gaye.
Auditor ka actual objective tha: standing receivables (jo abhi tak recover hone hain) par impairment / provision ki need ko assess karna.
🔹 Correct Population
Galat population: Already written-off balances.
Sahi population: Debtors ledger / AR balance listing (jo balance sheet date par outstanding hai).
👉 Focus hona chahiye remaining receivables par, kyunki risk yeh hai ke unme se kuch recoverable nahi hain.
🔹 Correct Audit Procedures
Aging Analysis
Outstanding balances ko aging buckets me divide karo (0–30 days, 31–60, 61–90, 90+).
Jitna purana debt, utna higher risk of non-recovery.
Subsequent Receipts Testing
Balance sheet date ke baad (subsequent period) me cash collections check karo.
Agar debtor ne paisa pay kar diya → receivable recoverable hai.
Agar nahi pay kiya → impairment ka risk.
External Confirmations
Selected debtors ko balance confirmations bhejo.
Positive confirmations se valuation aur existence dono assess hote hain.
Financial Condition Review
Major doubtful receivables ke liye debtor ki financial health assess karo.
Indicators: bankruptcy filings, liquidity position, payment history.
🔹 What NOT to Do
Already written-off balances par testing → sirf authorization/approval check ke liye relevant hota hai, provision assessment ke liye nahi.
Prior year comparison → valuation test nahi hai, completeness ke liye hota hai.
🔹 Sampling ka Standardized Design Steps
Define Purpose (Objective)
Kya test karna hai? → Test of Controls (TOC) ya Test of Details (TOD).
Example:
TOC → Credit approval system functioning?
TOD → Receivable balances existence/valuation?
Identify Population
Purpose ke according relevant population select karo.
Agar purpose ≠ population → poora sample flawed.
Define Deviation (TOC) or Misstatement (TOD)
TOC me deviation = control jaisa design hua tha waise perform nahi hua.
TOD me misstatement = recorded amount ≠ correct amount.
Determine Sample Size & Method
Risk, materiality aur tolerable error dekh kar.
Random, systematic, stratified methods.
Perform Testing & Evaluate
Sample execute karo.
Errors → project to population.
Aggregate & Conclude
Actual + Projected errors → SUOM.
Compare vs Materiality.
🔹 Key Distinction
Control Deficiency: Jab design hi flawed hai → control apne objective achieve nahi kar sakta.
Control Deviation: Design sahi hai, lekin operation me galti hui.
👉 Example:
Sales order me credit approval hona chahiye tha, lekin design hi missing hai → Deficiency.
Credit approval design tha, lekin ek instance me perform nahi hua → Deviation.
1. Control Deviation vs Control Deficiency
Control Deficiency → arises from Design Weakness (control not designed properly for its purpose).
Control Deviation → arises from Operating Ineffectiveness (control designed properly, but not performed as intended).
✅ Conclusion:
If design wrong → deficiency (no point in TOC).
If operating wrong → deviation (measured through sample testing).
2. Linking with Misstatements (Analogy)
Misstatement (amount errors) → compared with Materiality.
Control Deviations (control errors) → compared with Tolerable Rate of Deviation (TRD).
So, just like misstatements > materiality → FS materially misstated, control deviations > TRD → control not reliable.
3. Tolerable Rate of Deviation (TRD)
Defined: Maximum % of deviations auditor can accept before concluding that control is not operating effectively.
Key Principle:
Higher risk area → lower TRD (auditor less tolerant).
Lower risk area → higher TRD acceptable.
4. Example Case – Credit Approval
Population: 50 new customers.
Sample selected: 20 customers.
Procedure: Check whether credit limit approved.
Result:
Out of 20, 1 deviation found (no approval).
Deviation rate = 1/20 = 5%.
Assumption:
TRD = 10%.
Comparison:
Actual deviation rate (5%) < TRD (10%).
✅ Conclusion: Control is operating effectively (within tolerable range).
1. TRD (Tolerable Rate of Deviation) & Risk Link
Higher risk → TRD lower (kam deviation allow hogi)
Lower risk → TRD higher (zyada deviation allow hogi)
🔑 Formula-type thinking:
If Deviation % ≤ TRD → Controls operating effectively → Reliance possible.
If Deviation % > TRD → Controls not operating effectively → No reliance → Substantive testing ↑.
2. Risk Equation (Mind Map)
Inherent Risk (IR) → Pehle assess hota hai (without controls)
Control Risk (CR) → Depends on design + operating effectiveness
Audit Risk (AR) = IR × CR × Detection Risk (DR)
If Controls fail (high CR) → DR ko kam karna padega → More work, bigger sample.
3. Misstatement vs Deviation Recap
4. Worked Example (Credit Approval – Recap + Projection)
Population: 50 new customers
Sample: 20
Deviations found: 1
Sample Deviation Rate = 1/20 = 5%
Projected Deviation to population = 5% × 50 = 2.5 ≈ 5% (since count-based)
TRD set = 10%
Result: 5% < 10% → Reliance possible on control.

📚 LECTURE FULL EXPLANATION:
📌 Concept Breakdown
Projection in DOC (Deviation Observation Check):
Jab deviation Google rate se exceed kar jaye → phir projection nahi karni.
Focus: check karna hai ke population me deviation tolerable limit se zyada to nahi.
Terminology Clarity:
“Misstatement” → used jab test of details perform karte hain.
“Deviation” → used jab test of controls (TOC) karte hain.
Agar galat terminology exam me likhi → marks cut.
Tolerable Rate of Deviation (TRD):
Depend karta hai control design & effectiveness par.
Agar deviation % zyada → reliance kam ho jati hai → aur zyada tests of details perform karne padte hain.
Scenarios & Expected Deviation:
Large workforce (manual timesheet approval):
Expected deviation: High ✅
Tolerance: Low ✅
Large inventory count (semi-notes system):
Expected deviation: High → tolerance Low
Diverse portfolio of assets (new fixed asset tracking system):
Expected deviation: High (errors possible in new system)
Tolerance: Low
Cash transactions with multiple approval levels:
Good control design → expected deviation Low
Tolerance: High
Control Design vs. Inherent Risk:
Inherent risk high ho sakta hai, lekin agar control design strong hai → expected deviation kam hoti hai.
Example: multiple approval levels (2 lakh ka check ek banda approve, 5 lakh ka 2 log approve etc.) → design strong → expected deviation kam → tolerance zyada.
📊 Example Matching Table
Sampling Basics
Sampling = selection technique to test a portion of population.
Types of Sampling:
Statistical Sampling (uses mathematical techniques, avoids human bias).
Non-Statistical Sampling (relies on auditor’s judgment, bias possible).
Statistical Sampling
Based on mathematical/random techniques.
No human judgment involved → less bias.
Example: PTCL invoices (millions of customers → millions of line items). Auditor needs a sample.
Random Sampling
Use software (e.g., Excel, EY software) to select invoices randomly.
Formula: =RANDBETWEEN(1, N) where N = population size.
Example:
Population = 1 million invoices.
Required sample = 250 invoices.
Random generator selects numbers → invoices picked.
Ensures objectivity (no bias in which invoice is picked).
Systematic Sampling
Population arranged in order (ascending/descending).
Sample size determined by formula:
Interval (k) = Population ÷ Sample size
Select first random starting point, then every k-th item.
Example:
Branches = 400
Required sample = 25
Interval = 400 ÷ 25 = 16
If first selected branch = 7 → then 7, 23, 39, 55, … (every 16th branch).
Non-Statistical Sampling
Based on auditor’s judgment.
Risk: human bias.
Example:
Auditor selects invoices available at head office only (ignores remote branches).
Auditor picks northern area branches to “enjoy travel.”
Error detection risk ↑ because not representative.
1. Systematic Sampling
Definition: Selection of samples at regular intervals (fixed gap) after choosing the first random item.
Formula:
Sampling Interval = Population Size / Sample Size
Example: 400 branches ÷ 25 samples = interval of 16
Process:
Select first item randomly (e.g., branch no. 7)
Keep adding interval (16) → 7, 23, 39, 55 … until required samples selected
Key Point: No human judgment → No bias
2. Random vs. Systematic Sampling
Random Sampling:
Every item has equal chance of being selected.
Most preferred method (eliminates bias).
Systematic Sampling:
Uses fixed gaps → efficient, but some items may never be selected.
Both are Statistical Sampling Techniques.
3. Block Sampling
Definition: Selection of a whole block/sequence of items.
Use: Mainly for checking sequences (e.g., serial numbers).
Issue: Risk of bias, because selected block may not represent the whole population.
Improvement: If first item in block is random, bias reduces.
4. Statistical Sampling Methods
Random Sampling
Systematic Sampling
Block Sampling
Haphazard Sampling (non-statistical, bias risk)
5. Sample Size Determination
Important Distinction:
Sampling Technique = How to select samples
Sample Size = How many samples to select
Factors affecting Sample Size:
Tolerable Rate of Deviation (TRD):
Higher tolerance → Smaller sample
Lower tolerance → Larger sample
Expected Rate of Deviation (ERD):
Higher expectation of errors → Larger sample
Lower expectation → Smaller sample
Level of Assurance / Reliance on Controls:
More reliance → Larger sample size
Analytical Procedures:
If analytical results already provide assurance, sample size reduces
Population Size:
No direct effect on sample size (depends on risk, not population count)
